TL;DR: This paper presents an analysis of 42 confident gravitational-wave signals from the LIGO-Virgo-KAGRA detectors' fourth observing run, focusing on the remnants of binary mergers. It examines seven tests of general relativity (GR) involving the ringdown phase and potential echoes post-merger. Findings show overall consistency with GR; while hierarchical likelihood combination suggests GR's prediction is near the boundary of the credible region, updated from prior studies, but post-inclusion of a strong recent event (GW250114), the significance decreases, indicating no strong evidence against GR. Echoes are also not detected.

Motivation: The motivation is to rigorously test general relativity's predictions using gravitational-wave data, specifically examining the remnant properties of merging black holes to determine if the ringdown signals and post-ringdown echoes align with GR expectations, thus confirming or challenging the theory under extreme gravitational conditions.

Method: The study employs seven distinct tests: three analyzing the ringdown's consistency with Kerr black hole quasinormal mode spectra (two in time domain, one in frequency domain across the entire signal) and four testing for post-ringdown echoes. Data from GWTC-4.0's 42 confident events (two-detector confirmation, ≤1e-3 yr⁻¹ false alarm rate) and prior runs are used. Hierarchical Bayesian likelihood combination is applied to aggregate results, incorporating bootstrapping to address catalog size limitations.

Result: All tested remnants are consistent with GR predictions. Hierarchical analysis shows GR's prediction near the 98.6-99.3% credible region boundary (improved from GWTC-3.0's 93.8-94.9%). Including GW250114 reduces significance to ~92-96%, weakening evidence against GR. Echoes are undetected, with upper limits set for echo amplitudes (≤3% of ringdown amplitude).

Conclusion: Despite marginal boundary proximity in hierarchical analysis, GR remains supported by the data.
