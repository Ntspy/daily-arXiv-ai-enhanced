### [1] [Plummer Dark Matter Black Hole with Topological Defects: Shadow, Greybody Factors, Quasinormal Modes, and Thermodynamics](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.01255)
*Ahmad Al-Badawi,Faizuddin Ahmed,İzzet Sakallı*

Main category: gr-qc

TL;DR: This paper constructs a black hole solution embedded in a cored Plummer dark matter halo and a Letelier cloud of strings, analyzing various gravitational and thermodynamic properties.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To explore the effects of dark matter halos and string clouds on black hole physics, including spacetime structure, photon orbits, deflection angles, and thermodynamics.

Method: Modified Plummer-Schwarzschild metric with a string tension parameter, analytical calculations for photon sphere, shadow radii, deflection angles using Gauss-Bonnet theorem, and stability orbits. Scalar perturbations analyzed via effective potential, greybody factors, Hawking spectra, and quasinormal modes. Thermodynamic quantities derived and their dependences on parameters assessed.

Result: A single horizon exists for α <1, else a naked singularity. CoS tension α is the primary modifier across all observables, with Plummer density ρ0 providing secondary corrections. Negative heat capacity across all parameters negates Davies phase transitions.

Conclusion: The cloud of strings tension α dominants BH modifications, while dark matter halo effects are subdominant. Consistent results from multiple analyses validate the model's robustness against parameter influences.

Abstract: We construct a static, spherically symmetric black hole (BH) solution embedded in a cored Plummer dark matter (DM) halo and a Letelier cloud of strings (CoS). Starting from the Plummer-Schwarzschild metric of Senjaya et al.~\cite{Senjaya2026}, we incorporate the string-cloud tension parameter $α$ into the lapse function, obtaining $A(r) = h_{\rm Plummer}(r) - α$. The resulting spacetime admits a single, non-degenerate event horizon (EH) for $α< 1$ and a naked singularity for $α\ge 1$. We determine the photon sphere (PS) and BH shadow radii, compute the weak deflection angle via the Gauss-Bonnet theorem (GBT), and analyze the innermost stable circular orbit (ISCO). Scalar perturbations are studied through the effective potential, greybody factor (GF) bounds obtained from the Boonserm-Visser method, the Hawking emission spectrum, and quasinormal mode (QNM) frequencies computed with the WKB approximation. The thermodynamic analysis covers the Hawking temperature, Bekenstein-Hawking entropy, heat capacity, and Gibbs free energy; the heat capacity is found to be strictly negative for all parameter values, confirming the absence of any Davies-type phase transition. A consistent hierarchy emerges across all six analyses: the CoS tension $α$ governs the leading-order modifications to every observable, while the Plummer halo density $ρ_0$ provides a subdominant, additive correction.

### [2] [A framework for creating galaxy models in the geometry of the conservation group with dark matter halos and flat rotation curves](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.01270)
*Edward Lee Green*

Main category: gr-qc

TL;DR: The paper presents a new gravitational theory using orthonormal tetrads and an enlarged covariance group, introducing a modified Lagrangian with a curvature vector. It proposes a galaxy model divided into three regions (bulge, mesosphere, outside) to explain flat rotation curves without dark matter, using the radial acceleration relation.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To address the need for a dark matter-free explanation of galactic rotation curves and to explore a modified gravitational theory extending general relativity through an enlarged covariance group.

Method: Develops a theory using a conservation group superset of diffeomorphisms, formulates a free-field Lagrangian with curvature vector and source terms, derives spherically symmetric solutions, and models galaxy structures via three regions with specific density and isothermal conditions.

Result: The theory produces flat rotation curves in the mesosphere region with one free parameter. A continuous galaxy model combining all regions is constructed, aligning with observed radial acceleration relations.

Conclusion: The framework offers a viable alternative to dark matter by incorporating modified gravity effects through the proposed field equations and galaxy modeling approach, requiring further observational testing.

Abstract: Pandres has developed a theory which extends the geometrical structure of a real four-dimensional space-time via a field of orthonormal tetrads with an enlarged covariance group. This new group, called the conservation group, contains the group of diffeomorphisms as a proper subgroup. The free-field Lagrangian density involves only the curvature vector which is a vector which measures curvature. When massive objects are present a source term is added to this Lagrangian density. The weak-field approximation implies that gravitational waves travel at the speed of light. Spherically symmetric solutions for both the free field and the field with sources are found. In the free-field case, the field equations require nonzero stress-energy tensors. However, we find that for our model to be an acceptable model, we must have a source term in the Lagrangian. In our framework, we divide up the galaxy into three spherically symmetric regions: a baryonic matter-dominated central bulge, a dark matter-dominated mesosphere and an outside region where neither type dominates. Assuming the density of baryonic matter has a central cusp, we show how to model the bulge. Via an isothermal condition we find a model for the mesosphere and show this model implies flat rotation curves with one free parameter. The outside region is readily modeled via previously published results. The models for the bulge, mesosphere and outside region are combined into one continuous model. Using the radial acceleration relation we then show how a galaxy model may be set up for a rotationally supported galaxy.

### [3] [Is Gravity Always Enough to Yield a Classical Universe?](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.01283)
*Aurora Ireland*

Main category: gr-qc

TL;DR: The paper examines whether quantum features in the early universe could leave observable traces in cosmic structure, challenging the notion that all quantum effects become classical during inflation. It questions the sufficiency of traditional mechanisms and explores non-linear dynamics and phase-space analysis for detecting residual quantum signatures.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To address gaps in understanding the quantum-to-classical transition during inflation, particularly challenging the assumption that super-horizon dynamics and decoherence fully erase quantum structure. The goal is to investigate potential residual non-classical features in observable cosmological data.

Method: The authors analyze conventional assumptions about the quantum-to-classical transition, use phase-space analysis (via the Wigner function) to study non-linear dynamics of cosmological perturbations beyond slow-roll conditions. This approach aims to identify non-classical features in observables.

Result: Identifies that non-linear dynamics in cosmological perturbations can produce surviving non-classical features. Proposes the Wigner function method as a tool to detect these signatures, suggesting a pathway to observing quantum relics in cosmic structures.

Conclusion: Traditional views may underestimate residual quantum effects in cosmology; future analyses using phase-space techniques could reveal observable quantum imprints in cosmic structure, reshaping our understanding of the quantum-classical boundary in the universe's early evolution.

Abstract: The origin of cosmic structure is widely regarded as quantum, yet the Universe today appears classical. Standard lore attributes this to a "quantum-to-classical" transition on super-horizon scales during inflation. Gravity plays a central role: super-horizon dynamics squeeze quantum states, while the cosmological horizon enforces a system-environment split, leading to decoherence. But are these mechanisms always sufficient? We revisit this question, identifying assumptions and limitations in conventional arguments. We highlight recent work showing that beyond slow roll, non-linear dynamics of cosmological perturbations can generate non-classical features that may survive in observables. This raises the tantalizing possibility that quantum signatures may persist in cosmic structure. We propose a phase-space analysis based on the Wigner function as a concrete route to identifying and probing such signatures.

</details>
